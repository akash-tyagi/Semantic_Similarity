Microsoft CEO in D.C. for immigration, patents

By Tony Romm

12/8/14 8:48 PM EST

Updated 12/9/14 5:13 PM EST

Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella took his company’s case for immigration and patent reforms straight to the nation’s capital on Tuesday in a series of high-level meetings with congressional Republicans and Obama administration officials.

The Beltway blitz — commenced days before the end of a Congress that delivered mostly false starts for Microsoft and the entire tech industry — represents Nadella’s first major political overture since he took the company’s reins in February.

He huddled on Capitol Hill with House GOP leaders at the invitation of Rep. Cathy McMorris Rodgers (R-Wash.), she told POLITICO. He also looked to connect with Reps. Suzan DelBene (D) and Dave Reichert (R), who planned a meeting with the congressional delegation from Washington state, the home of Microsoft.

The CEO spoke with the Senate Republican High-Tech Task Force, led by Sen. Orrin Hatch, at the Utah lawmaker’s request. Nadella told the group, whose members include Sens. John Thune (S.D.) and Chuck Grassley (Iowa), that Microsoft needs Congress to address patent law “abuses” and offer new “legislative solutions” that would allow more high-skilled foreign workers in the United States, according to a source with knowledge of the meeting. And Nadella raised similar issues with Commerce Secretary Penny Pritzker as well as U.S. Trade Representative Michael Froman.

The company declined to make Nadella available for an interview this week.

Nadella’s Washington house call — where he also pitched Microsoft’s new cloud-computing tools for government — comes at a crucial political moment for the company and the broader tech industry.

Microsoft lobbied extensively in 2014 to advance legislation that would permit more high-skilled foreign workers, restrict NSA surveillance and clamp down on frivolous patent lawsuits. But the tech giant, along with its allies, watched all of those priorities falter on Capitol Hill. That has many industry leaders already positioning themselves for 2015.

The outreach also represents Nadella’s continued efforts to orient himself to the ways of Washington. The longtime Microsoft veteran is still relatively new to the political scene — but he has worked since his February promotion to the company’s top post to forge some of the strong D.C. bonds maintained by his well-connected predecessor, Steve Ballmer.

The very day Nadella became CEO of the tech giant, he connected by phone with Valerie Jarrett, one of President Barack Obama’s closest advisers, sources previously told POLITICO. He also has reached out over the past year to lawmakers like Sens. Mike Lee (R-Utah) and Marco Rubio (R-Fla.), as well as Rodgers, who said she met Nadella in the very office once occupied by Bill Gates.

“When I met with Satya earlier this year, I was taken by his vision for technology leadership and a new generation at Microsoft,” she said in a statement Monday. “I told him then that I looked forward to introducing him to my fellow members of leadership and the Conference, and I am thrilled that my colleagues tomorrow have the opportunity to meet him, as well.”

Nadella entered the world of political giving just this campaign cycle: He wrote his first check to Ro Khanna, a House Democratic candidate who had cultivated broad support among the Silicon Valley elite, like Google Executive Chairman Eric Schmidt and Yahoo CEO Marissa Mayer. Khanna ultimately lost his bid to unseat Rep. Mike Honda, a longtime Democratic incumbent.

Meanwhile, the Microsoft chief has confronted plenty of public relations challenges during his first year on the job. Nadella stoked criticism in July when the company announced it would lay off 18,000 employees. And he took flak in October after suggesting publicly that women should not ask for raises but instead have “faith in the system” — though Nadella quickly apologized for his remarks.

Appearing Tuesday on the Hill, though, Nadella found a receptive audience, according to the source with knowledge of the meeting. Hatch said he was “eager to work” with Nadella on high-skilled immigration, patent reform and trade secrets, the source said.
