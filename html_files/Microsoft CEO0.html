Shareholders approve Microsoft CEO’s $84 million pay package

By Rex Crum

Published: Dec 3, 2014 4:18 p.m. ET

Share

Software leader’s chief talks diversity and gets OK on pay package

By

RexCrum

Technology reporter

SAN FRANCISCO (MarketWatch)—Microsoft Corp. Chief Executive Satya Nadella capped a successful initial 10 months on the job Wednesday by hosting his first annual meeting as CEO, with shareholders approving his pay package valued at more than $84 million.

Nadella’s compensation consists mostly of grants of Microsoft stock MSFT, +0.67%  that vest over time.

Nadella has overseen a renewed enthusiasm for Microsoft’s stock since he took over from Steve Ballmer as CEO on Feb. 4. Microsoft’s shares have risen more than 32% during his tenure to trade at $48.27 on Wednesday. The stock reached a multi-year high of $50.05 on Nov. 14.

Nadella used some of his time on stage at the meeting to highlight what he has called the “mobile first, cloud first” direction that Microsoft is pursuing. Microsoft is trying to “build on the foundation of Bill [Gates] and Steve [Ballmer],” he said. As part of his strategy, Nadella announced plans during the summer to trim 18,000 jobs from the company.

“We’re making smart and disciplined decisions and taking bold action to step forward,” Nadella said.

Nadella also reminded the attendees that 2015 will mark the 40th anniversary of Microsoft’s founding, and addressed the matter of diversity in the company’s workforce.

Rev. Jesse Jackson was in attendance at the meeting, and was the first to address Nadella, Chief Financial officer Amy Hood, and other Microsoft executives when the gathering’s question and answer session began. Jackson urged Nadella and Microsoft to take more steps to hire minorities, especially in areas of technological development.

“We are focused on getting a diverse talent base and retaining them as they grow inside the company,” said Nadella, who is a native of India. “You have to have a culture of inclusiveness.”

FBR Capital Markets analyst Daniel Ives, said Nadella looked like a man intent on restoring Microsoft to its former greatness.

“I thought it was a clear difference from years past,” Ives said. “Nadella and Microsoft are more comfortable in their market positioning, growth and strategy, given the success they have had in 2014.”

Microsoft shareholders also voted in favor of the company’s current slate of 10 directors, including Charles Noski, Helmut Panke, non-executive chairman John Thompson, and co-founder Bill Gates, who stepped down as board chairman in February and now serves as a technical adviser to Nadella. Shareholders also elected four other board members—John Stanton, Charles Scharf, Teri List-Stoll and Mason Morfit.

A company proposal to ratify Deloitte & Touche as Microsoft’s independent auditor was also approved by shareholders, while a shareholder proposal that would give Microsoft stock owners more of a say in selecting the company’s board members was rejected.
