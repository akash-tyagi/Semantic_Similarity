The five best books Bill Gates read in 2014

By Silvia Ascarelli

Published: Dec 25, 2014 3:16 p.m. ET

Share

By

SilviaAscarelli

Senior news editor
Slide 1 of 7

NEW YORK (MarketWatch) — Bill Gates is back with a list of his favorite reads from 2014.

Unlike many other best books lists, the co-founder of Microsoft Corp. MSFT, -1.21% isn’t restricting himself to those published this year, just those he particularly enjoyed during the past 12 months.

Like many of us, he falls behind on his reading list and rereads old favorites.

Still, he does include one book that isn’t out until the end of the year (he was given an advance copy).

What’s he liking? Most of this year’s reading, he notes on his blog, touched on economics and business. But he does include one novel on his list of the best.

Keep reading for his picks. And for those who want to focus on great books for investors, here’s a (non-Bill Gates) list.

Also read: How to really invest like a billionaire

Slide 2 of 7

“Business Adventures”

This book by John Brooks might look familiar — it made Gates’s list of six books to read this summer. The book is really a collection of 12 articles, and Gates says he reread the chapter on Xerox Corp. XRX, -1.07%  this year.

Bonus: The book has been recommended by Warren Buffett as well.

The book had been out of print, but after Gates recommended the book this summer, it was quickly brought back and now is among the best-selling books on Amazon.com Inc. AMZN, +0.02%  

Slide 3 of 7

“Capital in the Twenty-First Century”

This controversial Thomas Piketty book was one of the year’s best sellers on economics, and Gates says he spent an hour chatting with Piketty about it. “As I told him, although I have concerns about some of his secondary points and policy prescriptions, I agree with his most important conclusions: inequality is a growing problem and that governments should play a role in reducing it,” Gates writes in his blog.

Also read: Diana Furchtgott-Roth says Piketty’s cure for income inequality hurts the poor and How to invest if Piketty is right on growing inequality. Plus: Paul Farrell on who’s actually read Piketty

Slide 4 of 7

“How Asia Works”

Gates says the message from this book, by Joe Studwell, has him thinking about what parts of the Asian model can apply to Africa. The book, he says, offers compelling answers to how some Asian countries achieve sustained, high growth, and others don’t.

Also read: You aren’t investing in Africa — and you’re missing out.

Slide 5 of 7

“The Rosie Effect”

Gates said this summer that he loved Graeme Simsion’s “The Rosie Project.” This sequel to that novel is hilarious and thought-provoking, he says.

But you can’t put it under the tree. This one comes out after Christmas.

Slide 6 of 7

“Making the Modern World: Materials and Dematerialization”

Turns out author Vaclav Smil is a Gates favorite. “There is no author whose books I look forward to more,” he wrote last year about this professor emeritus at the University of Manitoba in Canada. Here’s what Gates is saying about this one: “If anyone tries to tell you we’re using fewer materials, send him this book.”

Another Smil book that Gates has singled out in the past is “Harvesting the Biosphere: What We Have Taken From Nature.”

Want another reading list? Farnam Street offers a compilation of books recommended by billionaires, chess prodigies, authors and more.
